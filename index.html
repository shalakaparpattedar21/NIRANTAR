Learn About Physiotherapy

Expand your understanding of physiotherapy, common conditions, and how to take charge of your health and recovery.

Definition & Scope

Physiotherapy is a healthcare discipline using physical methods to restore, maintain, and maximise movement and functional ability.

Read more
It encompasses assessment, diagnosis, and treatment of injury, disease, or disability. Physiotherapists work across hospitals, private clinics, sports settings, and community care to help patients of all ages regain independence and quality of life.

How It Works

Physiotherapy works by addressing the root cause of pain through targeted movement, manual therapy, and rehabilitation.

Read more
Techniques stimulate the body's natural healing processes. Exercise strengthens weakened muscles, manual therapy improves joint mobility, and patient education helps manage conditions for the long term.

Who Can Benefit?

Anyone experiencing pain, movement limitations, or recovering from surgery or injury can benefit from physiotherapy.

Read more
From athletes recovering from sports injuries to elderly patients managing arthritis, from stroke survivors relearning movement to office workers with chronic back pain — physiotherapy offers evidence-based solutions for diverse needs.

Manual Therapy

Hands-on techniques including joint mobilisation, manipulation, and soft tissue release to reduce pain and restore movement.

Read more
Manual therapy targets restricted joints and tight muscles directly. Therapists use precise forces to improve joint mechanics, reduce muscle tension, and modulate pain signals — often providing immediate relief.

Electrotherapy

TENS, ultrasound, and laser therapy to reduce pain, inflammation, and stimulate tissue healing at a cellular level.

Read more
Electrical and energy-based modalities accelerate the healing process. Particularly useful in the early stages of injury management when active exercise may be too painful to perform safely.

Dry Needling

Fine needles inserted into trigger points to release muscle tension and relieve chronic pain patterns.

Read more
Unlike acupuncture, dry needling targets myofascial trigger points based on musculoskeletal anatomy. It can rapidly deactivate painful knots, restore range of motion, and reduce referred pain patterns in chronic conditions.

Lower Back Pain

The most common musculoskeletal complaint, affecting up to 80% of adults at some point in their lives.

Read more
Causes range from muscle strain and disc herniation to poor posture and sedentary lifestyle. Physiotherapy addresses the root cause through core strengthening, postural correction, manual therapy, and ergonomic education.

Knee Injuries

ACL tears, meniscal damage, patellofemoral pain, and osteoarthritis are among the most common knee conditions.

Read more
Knee rehabilitation focuses on quadriceps and hamstring strengthening, proprioception training, and biomechanical correction. Post-surgical rehab follows a structured protocol to safely restore full function.

Shoulder Problems

Rotator cuff tears, frozen shoulder, and impingement syndrome frequently affect both active and sedentary individuals.

Read more
Shoulder rehabilitation requires careful scapular stabilisation, rotator cuff strengthening, and joint mobilisation. Early intervention prevents chronic stiffness and the need for surgical management.

Core Stabilisation

Deep abdominal and spinal exercises forming the foundation of injury prevention and back pain management.

Read more
Begin with diaphragmatic breathing and transverse abdominis activation. Progress to bird-dog, dead bug, and plank variations. Core stability underpins every movement pattern in daily life and sport.

Balance & Proprioception

Exercises that retrain the nervous system's sense of body position — critical after any joint injury.

Read more
Single-leg stands, wobble board training, and perturbation exercises rebuild the neuromuscular control that protects joints from re-injury. Especially vital for ankles, knees, and post-surgical recovery.

Stretching & Flexibility

Static, dynamic, and PNF stretching techniques to restore full range of motion and prevent recurring injury.

Read more
Proprioceptive Neuromuscular Facilitation is one of the most effective stretching techniques, using the body's reflexes to achieve greater flexibility gains than passive stretching alone.

Ergonomic Workspace

Desk, chair, monitor, and keyboard setup that supports spinal alignment during long work hours.

Read more
Monitor at eye level, elbows at 90 degrees, feet flat on the floor, and lumbar support engaged. Take a five-minute movement break every 45 minutes. These small changes prevent chronic pain and cumulative strain injuries.

Sleep Posture

Sleeping position significantly impacts spinal health, neck pain, and overall recovery from injuries.

Read more
Side-lying with a pillow between the knees reduces spinal load. Back sleeping with a pillow under the knees supports the lumbar curve. Avoid stomach sleeping — it increases neck rotation and lumbar compression during rest.

Hydration & Nutrition

Optimal tissue healing requires adequate hydration, protein intake, and anti-inflammatory dietary choices.

Read more
Collagen synthesis requires Vitamin C. Omega-3 fatty acids reduce systemic inflammation. Adequate protein supports muscle repair. Dehydration reduces intervertebral disc height and joint lubrication quality.
